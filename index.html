1. Overview

The Ecological Crisis

Since 2007, a mangrove ecosystem along the southern coast of Selangor has been subject to long-term, cumulative, and structural degradation. This process is not a sudden event but a clearly traceable ecological decline trajectory that has remained largely unaddressed over the years.

  • 2007–2017: Large-scale white sand encroachment, directly burying intertidal sediments
  • 2007–2025: At least 46.8% of the original ~22.6 hectares permanently lost
  • Sand encroachment rate: ~540 m²/year over 18 years
  • 2025–2026: Engineering interventions without ecological assessment accelerate collapse

Study Location

The study area is located along the southern coast of Selangor at Kuala Langat, Tanjung Sepat (Pantai Cunang), bounded to the north by Batu Laut and to the south by Sungai Pelak.

This area occupies a key ecological position at the river mouth–coast interface:

  • Coastal protection
  • Supporting local fisheries
  • Providing refuge for diverse species
  • Irreplaceable component of regional coastal systems

2. Study Area Overview & Methodology

1

Spatial Partitioning

This study divides the mangroves into two major zones:

  • Western Zone: A001, A002, A003
  • Eastern Zone: B001
2

White Sand Source Analysis

Official explanations attribute sand to natural geomorphological evolution following the 2004 tsunami. However, considering regional development contexts, human influences cannot be excluded.

3

Temporal Demarcation

The study uses 2025 as a temporal demarcation to compare coastal structure before and after human engineering interventions.

4

Data Sources

  • Satellite remote sensing imagery
  • Field surveys and observations
  • Biodiversity records
  • Hydrological monitoring

3. Major Findings

Coastal Structure Characteristics Before 2025

Seaward Soft Mangrove Belt

Directly exposed to tidal and wave action, functioning as a buffer to dissipate wave energy, stabilize sediments, and provide shelter for intertidal species.

White Sand Deposition Zone

Located behind mangroves, elevated relative to inland mangroves, preventing seawater from reaching interior areas during high tide.

High-Risk Zones

B001 and A001 zones exhibited the highest ecological risk from persistent sand encroachment and westward advancement.

Failed Natural Regeneration

Mangrove seedlings struggled to establish; viviparous propagules rapidly decayed under harsh conditions.

Coastal Structure Changes After 2025

Critical Turning Point: Starting in 2025, the coastal structure underwent drastic human-induced modification

Engineering Intervention

RM7.9 million recirculating diaphragm upgrade project (June 21, 2024 – July 21, 2026) including 375m rock revetment, training walls, and beach cultivation.

Vegetation Destruction

Newly deposited sand rapidly buries and kills vegetation. Previously surviving mangroves show pronounced mortality and structural collapse.

Hydrological Severance

Seawalls and sand deposits block all potential seawater pathways. Tidal exchange nearly completely halted.

Ecological Consequences

Post-2025 engineering has systematically undermined the operational foundation of the entire ecosystem.

White Sand Advancement Trend and Regional Ecological Risk

Advancement Rate and Pattern

Before 2025: Sand consistently advanced westward from B001 zone at accelerating rates. 2025 Status: Sand front reached boundary between A001 and A002.

Area Expansion Data

2025 White Sand Area: 9,752 m². Net Increase Rate: ~540 m²/year. Growth Pattern: Non-linear with early rapid initiation.

Beyond Natural Processes

This expansion over 18 years exceeds scale explained by natural sedimentation and directly threatens mangrove stability.

Regional Chain Reaction Risk

Current engineering presents local and regional risks, potentially causing irreversible mangrove damage along southern Selangor coast.

Biodiversity and Ecological Significance

Flora - Mangrove Species

3-4 mangrove species recorded: Rhizophora, Bruguiera, Avicennia, and Sonneratia, indicating structurally complex and mature ecosystem.

Avifauna (Birds)

Herons, egrets, fish eagles (Haliaeetus leucogaster), brahminy kites (Haliastur indus), and other raptors. Hornbills and kingfishers observed historically.

Pelagic & Benthic Fauna

Fish species, jellyfish, bivalves (mussels, clams) indicating stable water exchange and productive benthic ecosystems.

Intertidal Macrofauna

Crustaceans: Mudskippers, mantis shrimps, crabs (various species), shrimp. Mollusks: Shellfish, snails. Echinoderms: Sea stars, sea cucumbers. Core of energy flow and food web.

Megafauna & Protected Species

Mammals: Macaques, monitor lizards. Chelonians: Horseshoe crabs (Tachypleus gigas, Carcinoscorpius rotundicauda) - highly protected intertidal species, indicator of ecosystem health. Sea turtles occasionally recorded.

Conclusion: Ecosystem exhibits high biodiversity and functional diversity. High species richness indicates systemic collapse, not simple vegetation loss - the ecosystem was thriving before 2025.

Species Distribution Differences and Hydrological Imbalance

Key Observation

A001 & A002: Live organisms mainly recorded. B001 outer areas: Numerous deceased individuals. Pattern reflects severe hydrological imbalance.

Biological Sink Zones

Abnormal water exchange prevents energy dissipation. Organisms cannot return to suitable habitats. Seedling establishment halted.

Cascading Impact Risk

Engineering may alter hydrodynamics affecting other zones. Mangroves depend on spatial continuity - systemic collapse likely.

6. Conclusions and Recommendations

Research Summary

This study documents the progressive structural degradation of a mangrove ecosystem over nearly two decades. What began as gradual white sand encroachment evolved into a compounded ecological crisis, intensified by large-scale engineering interventions introduced after 2025 without comprehensive ecological assessment.

Critical Findings

  • Geomorphological Erosion: East-to-west sand advancement systematically compressed mangrove habitats
  • Hydrological Severance: Post-2025 engineering fundamentally altered coastal system functionality
  • Ecological Indicators: Biological sink zones and increased mortality signal systemic collapse
  • Regional Risk: Potential chain reactions affecting broader southern Selangor coast

Urgent Recommendations

1

Independent Ecological Assessment

Conduct transparent ecological impact assessments before continued engineering

2

Regional Hydrodynamic Modeling

Evaluate cross-boundary sediment and hydrodynamic risks

3

Hydrological Connectivity Restoration

Immediately restore partial tidal exchange

4

Sediment Control

Implement sediment management with urgency and precision

5

Conservation Status

Formally recognize as conservation-priority coastal ecosystem

The Present Moment

This crisis remains ongoing. Ecological systems retain limited windows of recoverability if structural integrity is restored in time.

If no intervention grounded in scientific evidence is undertaken, the trajectory observed from 2007 to 2026 will likely culminate in irreversible regional mangrove collapse. Conversely, if hydrological restoration and sediment control are implemented with urgency and precision, coexistence between tourism infrastructure and mangrove conservation remains within the realm of possibility.

The present moment represents not an endpoint, but a threshold.
